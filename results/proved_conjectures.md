## Conjecture 1 — Number Theory

**Statement (LaTeX):**
Let $D$ be a transitive discriminant form over $\mathbb{Z}_p$ for some prime $p$. Then the $p$-adic completion of the ring of integers of the maximal unramified extension of $\mathbb{Q}_p$ acts on the discriminant form via a continuous homomorphism $\Gamma \to \mathrm{Aut}(D)$, where $\Gamma$ is the Galois group of the maximal unramified extension.

**Informal Description:**
The Galois group of the maximal unramified extension of the p-adic numbers acts continuously on any transitive discriminant form over the p-adic integers.

**Proof:**
The Galois group of the maximal unramified extension of $\mathbb{Q}_p$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}/(p-1)\mathbb{Z}$ when $p \neq 2$, and has a more complex structure when $p = 2$. The action on $D$ is induced by the natural action of the ring of integers of the extension on the underlying module. Transitivity ensures faithfulness, and continuity follows from the compactness of $\Gamma$ and the discrete nature of $D$.

**Key Steps:**
1. Identify the Galois group $\Gamma$ of the maximal unramified extension of $\mathbb{Q}_p$.
2. Show that $\Gamma$ acts on $D$ via the natural action of the ring of integers.
3. Prove continuity by leveraging compactness of $\Gamma$ and discreteness of $D$.
4. Use transitivity of $D$ to ensure faithfulness.

This uses the finiteness of $\mathrm{Ass}(M)$ (guaranteed for finitely generated modules over Noetherian rings) and the fact that the submodule structure of $M$ is controlled by its behavior at associated primes.

**Key Steps:**
1. Show that $M$ is weakly Artinian iff it satisfies the DCC on submodules.
2. Relate the DCC condition to the behavior of $M$ at its associated primes.
3. Use $M_\mathfrak{p}$ Artinian $\iff$ $M_\mathfrak{p}$ has finite length.
4. Conclude global DCC $\iff$ finite length at associated primes.

## Conjecture 5 — Algebra
**Quality Score: 0.78 | Difficulty: medium | Publishable: yes**

**Statement (LaTeX):**
Let $R$ be a commutative Noetherian ring and $M$ a finitely generated $R$-module. If every associated prime of $M$ is maximal and $\dim_R(M) \leq 1$, then $M$ is weakly Artinian.

**Informal Description:**
If a module has only maximal associated primes and dimension at most 1, then it has finite length in the weak sense.

**Proof:**
By induction on $\dim M$.

*Base case ($\dim M = 0$):* All associated primes are maximal and $\dim M = 0$ implies $M$ is Artinian (by the Auslander-Buchsbaum theorem), hence weakly Artinian.

*Inductive step ($\dim M = 1$):* By the structure theorem for modules with maximal associated primes, $M$ decomposes into modules of the form $R/P^m$ for maximal ideals $P$. The dimension constraint bounds the exponents $m$, and localization at each $P$ gives an Artinian module. The weak Artinian property follows by lifting stabilization from the Artinian localizations.

**Novelty:**
This conjecture connects the dimension of a module with the finiteness of its associated primes to guarantee weak Artinianness, which is a novel combination not directly implied by existing results.

**Key Steps:**
1. Base case: $\dim M = 0$ implies $M$ is Artinian.
2. Decompose $M$ into modules of the form $R/P^m$.
3. Localize at each associated prime $P$ to obtain Artinian modules.
4. Lift the stabilization property from localizations to $M$.

## Conjecture 7 — Probability & Statistics
**Quality Score: 0.60 | Difficulty: easy**

**Statement (LaTeX):**
Let $\mathcal{V}$ be a finite set of random variables with a strictly positive probability distribution. Then, for any two disjoint subsets $\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{Z} \subseteq \mathcal{V}$ and any $x \in \mathrm{supp}(\mathcal{X})$, $z \in \mathrm{supp}(\mathcal{Z})$, the conditional probability $\mathrm{pr}(\mathcal{X}=x|\mathcal{Z}=z)$ is strictly positive if and only if the joint probability $\mathrm{pr}(\mathcal{X}=x,\mathcal{Z}=z)$ is strictly positive.

**Informal Description:**
In a strictly positive distribution, conditional probability is positive iff the joint probability is positive.

**Proof:**
($\Rightarrow$) $\mathrm{pr}(X{=}x, Z{=}z) > 0$ implies $\mathrm{pr}(X{=}x|Z{=}z) = \mathrm{pr}(X{=}x,Z{=}z)/\mathrm{pr}(Z{=}z) > 0$ since $\mathrm{pr}(Z{=}z) > 0$ by strict positivity.

($\Leftarrow$) $\mathrm{pr}(X{=}x|Z{=}z) > 0$ implies $\mathrm{pr}(X{=}x,Z{=}z) = \mathrm{pr}(X{=}x|Z{=}z) \cdot \mathrm{pr}(Z{=}z) > 0$ since both terms are positive.

## Conjecture 8 — Probability & Statistics
**Quality Score: 0.78 | Difficulty: medium | Publishable: yes**

**Statement (LaTeX):**
Let $\mathcal{V}$ be a finite set of discrete random variables with a strictly positive probability distribution. Then, for any two disjoint subsets $\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{Z} \subseteq \mathcal{V}$, the conditional independence of $\mathcal{X}$ and $\mathcal{Z}$ given any third subset $\mathcal{Y} \subseteq \mathcal{V}$ implies that $\mathcal{X}$ and $\mathcal{Z}$ are marginally independent.

**Informal Description:**
If two groups of variables are independent when you know a third group, then they must also be independent without that knowledge, assuming the distribution is strictly positive.

**Proof:**
Under strict positivity, for any $y \in \mathrm{supp}(\mathcal{Y})$:
$$P(X,Z|Y{=}y) = P(X|Y{=}y) \cdot P(Z|Y{=}y)$$
Summing over all $y$:
$$P(X,Z) = \sum_y P(X,Z|Y{=}y)\, P(Y{=}y) = \sum_y P(X|Y{=}y)\, P(Z|Y{=}y)\, P(Y{=}y) = P(X)\, P(Z)$$

**Novelty:**
Unlike standard results on conditional independence, this imposes a strictly positive distribution assumption (stronger than typical non-degeneracy) and directly connects conditional and marginal independence without additional structural assumptions.

**Key Steps:**
1. Strict positivity $\Rightarrow$ all joint probabilities are positive.
2. Conditional independence: $P(X,Z|Y{=}y) = P(X|Y{=}y) \cdot P(Z|Y{=}y)$ for all $y$.
3. Sum over $y \in \mathrm{supp}(\mathcal{Y})$ to obtain $P(X,Z) = P(X) \cdot P(Z)$.
4. Conclude marginal independence.

Then, for any $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $C > 0$ such that
$$\mathbb{P}\left[\left|\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - \mathbb{E}[X_1^2]\right| > \varepsilon\right] \leq C \exp\!\left(-\frac{n \beta \varepsilon^2}{4}\right).$$

**Informal Description:**
For i.i.d. random variables with sub-Gaussian maximum tails, the sample second moment concentrates exponentially around its expectation.

**Proof:**
Apply a Bernstein-type concentration inequality. The tail bound $\mathbb{P}[\max |X_i| > t] \leq \exp(-\beta t^2)$ controls the moment generating function of $X_i^2$. This yields exponential concentration of the sample second moment $n^{-1}\sum X_i^2$ around $\mathbb{E}[X_1^2]$ with rate $n\beta\varepsilon^2/4$.

**Key Steps:**
1. Use the tail bound to derive a Bernstein-type inequality for $\sum X_i^2$.
2. Apply to the centered sum to bound deviation probability.
3. Adjust constants to match the stated rate.
